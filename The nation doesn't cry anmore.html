The nation doesn't cry anmore

by: Aafreen

The nation doesnâ€™t cry anymore. She has no tears left to shed now. All she has currently is a cheek left dry from the frequent factitious wiping away of the pools of water that collect in her eyelids as she sees the state she has now been degraded to. She still allows the men walking on her yet habitable lands to talk about her glorious past with much grandeur as they continue to catalyse her destruction and smear her once regal past in distinct hues of downfall.

Like an expecting mother who loses her newborn to the winds of fate, she too laments over the broken expectations of what her child could have become. She often looks at her marvellous past and agonises over the shared stories of her religious anecdotes, formatively becoming seeds of disharmony rather than plants of hope. She always believed that the stories so culturally knit into her breathable fabric would become beacons of hope and togetherness in times of disharmony and strife. But all she hears today, as she sits bosomed by her nostalgia, are the gut-wrenching sounds of oppression.

The banshee in her wails for all the lives she believes will be lost if her supposed devotees do not mend their ways. Her shrieks speak of inherent pain, but the alleged nationalist mavens label the same soul-searing shrieks as songs of betterment and development. With every new case of lynching, they say, she edges closer and closer to discovering a new melody, a new piece of a renaissance. With every cow saved and every man put down, they say, she stands more and more near the precipice of becoming unrivalled. Ironically, her rivals themselves trumpet the alleged ways that can be used to make her a superpower.

She regarded herself as a strong competitor in the race to become a superpower. However, currently, she is too engrossed in keeping the diverse threads of her social fabric together. She is highly engaged in protecting the individual lines that intermittently mingle to give her an identity of a multiculturally diverse nation. She weeps like a mother with a sick child, devastated yet hopeful. Unbeknownst to what shall happen next, she prays to those who call her their own and the god they fight for. She neither rings temple bells nor ties sacred threads to the walls of an establishment when seeking blessings. However, all she does is seek a chance to be something more with all her purity and zeal. Something unlike what she sees herself becoming every day.

As her institutions lose their credibility, she wishes that there were more zealous people who stood by the values that she breathes for. As she sees her years-long history of sacrifice and blood turning to ash, she feels sorry for all the potential getting drained. With moisture in her eyes but no tears left to shed, she looks at the immense talent she loses as her alleged patriots drive the thinkers to the edge of resettlement. The brain drain happening regularly drives her mad, but she is declared of unsound mind every time she raises her voice. Her muffled sounds speak of agony, but it falls on either the deaf ears of the alleged patriots or on the scared ears of the general population.

With every report concealed, she witnesses yet another truth being withheld and then becoming a mystery: a mystery worthy of being kept away from the light of the day. She observes as another fact meant to be documented slowly turns into dust. As she blows away the dust, she sees yet another story kept tightly packed in the yellowing paged of the binders. Though no more sealed cover jurisprudence shall be allowed, say the courts, she wonders how many of the aggrieved have time and money to fight a mighty ghost that has all the power and resources.

At this juncture in her life, she is unsure about whether she should hold back all that she has repressed for a very long time or die a death worthy of a martyr after speaking her heart out. She still, after years of burning in inflicted misery, holds hope close to her bosom as she moves forward. She does so not because she has too many good incidents to hold onto but because she, as a nation, has grown up believing in the strength of hope.
